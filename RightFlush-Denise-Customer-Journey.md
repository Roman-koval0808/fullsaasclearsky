# The RightFlush Customer Journey — Denise's Story

A continuous narrative tracing one prospect through the ClearSky platform, chaptered by pipeline stage. Built incrementally to surface each major system piece as it becomes relevant to her, in the order a real visitor would actually encounter it. Setting: RightFlush Plumbing (Timmins, ON), the canonical demo client. Companion to the abbreviated "one booked job" trace in `specs/00-INTEGRATION-SPEC.md` §11 — this version stops to explain each mechanism instead of compressing it to a sentence.

## For Serhii — how to read this

This isn't a spec. It's a test harness: we run one simulated customer through the whole platform, start to finish, and narrate what should happen at every step according to the specs as written. Writing it as a continuous story instead of a checklist is deliberate — it's the only way we've found to surface timing questions, "who approves this" questions, and "what happens when a human misses a step" questions that a component-by-component spec doesn't force you to confront. The specs are what's being tested; this story is the test.

A few formatting conventions that matter:

- **Plain narrative prose** describes what happens. **Italicized `→` asides** step out of the story to cite the exact mechanism — file, section, or line number — that justifies it. Treat those citations as claims: if you're about to build against one, open the cited file and confirm it still says that, don't take the story's word for it alone.
- **`ACT-*` / `SIG-*` IDs** are references to the real Action Library and Signal Library (`ClearSky_A2P_Developer_Spec.md`, `002_seed_rules.sql`, `004_40_signals_seed.sql`). When a chapter says an Action "fires," it means the story is asserting that's what the current spec says should happen — not that it's confirmed built and working in your repo. If you've already implemented something that behaves differently, that's a conflict worth flagging back to us, not something to silently reconcile in either direction.
- **"LOCKED [date]"** marks a decision Rory has explicitly confirmed. Treat these as settled — safe to build against.
- **"Proposed," "not yet in the locked library," "tracker #NN"** marks the opposite: something invented in the course of telling the story because no existing Action covered what was needed, flagged rather than treated as real. These need your review (does the ID collide with something you've already built? does the shape make sense?) before anyone builds against them.
- **"Gap," "open," "flagged"** marks a hole in the specs the story ran into and didn't paper over — a place where telling a realistic story required an answer that doesn't exist yet. These need a product/eng conversation, not an assumption.

**Single consolidated list:** `specs/clearsky-open-decisions-tracker.md` has every open decision, every proposed-but-unbuilt Action, and every known gap from both this story and Barry's (`customer-journeys/01-clearsky-home-01John.md`), in flat table form with priority tags — read that first if you want the list without the narrative. This story and Barry's are companion pieces: his starts from a different entry point and independently hits some of the same walls (the appointment/technician-capacity system, most notably) — that's confirming a real gap, not a duplicate finding. If you want the punch-list version — new Action IDs to review, what's blocking these stories vs. general backlog, what's already locked — that's `specs/clearsky-serhii-issues-list.md`, no narrative required.

Ground rules behind why some things stay unresolved instead of guessed past: see `CLAUDE.md` at the repo root — "work against the specs, don't invent architecture," "flag conflicts rather than silently resolving," "decisions lock when Rory confirms."

## Chapter 1 — A Woman Who Doesn't Know We Exist Yet

**Months before she ever types RightFlush's name into anything**, the machinery that will eventually find her is already running — and it has never heard of her, because it isn't built to.

It's late February. Two hours south, Sudbury's bathroom-renovation keyword searches climb past 200% of their 90-day baseline. Timmins couldn't have caught this on its own — its own search volume for a keyword this specific is too thin; a good week and a real demand spike would look identical in the data. Sudbury's big enough that the spike is actually legible, and similar enough to Timmins — same climate, same aging housing stock, same trades economy — that it's a trustworthy stand-in. ContentRadar catches the spike and fires a flag: *worth publishing on, here's the subject matter to explore.*

*→ Still Stream B — aggregate, no individual attached. The reason it works isn't that trends travel from Sudbury to Timmins on any schedule — they don't. It's that Timmins is too small to hear itself coming: at its own search volume, a real demand spike and a good week look identical. Sudbury is large enough that the same spike is legible, and similar enough — climate, aging housing stock, trades economy — to be a trustworthy stand-in for what Timmins homeowners are also beginning to think about. RightFlush publishes early because the signal is real and the topic doesn't go stale fast — not off any countdown.*

Nobody at RightFlush writes this post from scratch. A ClearSky support person picks up the flag, checks that it's a real signal worth acting on, and hands it to the pipeline that actually produces the content: an AI queries DataForSEO for "bathroom renovation" and pulls the five strongest existing stories on the topic — not to copy them, but to see what's already been said and said well. An AI author — RightFlush's voice and style already locked in from setup, not generic — reads all five, takes the strongest ideas across them, and writes something new: *"Signs It's Time to Renovate Your Bathroom (And What It Actually Costs in Timmins)."*

It doesn't publish yet. It goes to Sarah Jenkins — the ClearSky consultant assigned to RightFlush — for review. (RightFlush's owner is James Dredhart; the approval gate is ClearSky's consultant, not the contractor.)

*→ This is the non-negotiable in practice: public-facing content requires human approval, no override path, nothing posts to any external channel automatically. It doesn't matter that the topic came from a statistically clean signal or that the draft was well-written — Sarah reads it before it goes anywhere, same gate as if a customer's negative review needed a response. She approves it. It goes up on `/blog`, ahead of the curve.*

**Her name is Denise.** She's been quietly annoyed at her bathroom for about a year — the grout, the tub that's a chore to get in and out of, the general sense that it's the one room in the house she doesn't like showing people. She hasn't called anyone. She hasn't even really "started looking." One night, half-watching TV, phone in hand, she types something like *"bathroom renovation cost Timmins"* into Google.

RightFlush's blog post — the one ContentRadar helped surface weeks ahead of when she'd have found it otherwise — is right there.

She reads it. Skims, really. Scrolls about halfway down. Closes the tab after about 35 seconds without clicking anything.

*→ Two separate systems wake up off the exact same handful of events — one tracking WHO she is, one tracking WHAT SHE WANTS. Worth walking both, because they don't share logic, only inputs.*

*→ **The identity side (§4.5a).** Event validity passes — obviously not a bot. The moment she clears the 10-second floor, the system checks the DB for her phone's fingerprint before creating anything. No match; this device has never been seen. A record gets created right then, not deferred until she does something more. Cookie and fingerprint are the only two things it knows about her — no name, no email, nothing. She's **Tier 2B, immediately.** Confirmed real. Zero identifiers. Log and hold.*

*→ **The intent side (§3.2, real numbers off the live `/blog` page).** Every one of those same events also feeds a running Engagement Score and a bucket decision — a completely different calculation, same trigger:*

| Event | Delta | Bucket tag | Running score |
|---|---|---|---|
| `page_load` | +3 | research | **3** |
| `scroll_25` | +3 | research | **6** |
| `scroll_50` | +5 | research | **11** |
| `dwell_30` | +4 | research | **15** |

*→ The actual scoring code (`firePixel()`, live on every page) is two lines: `score = min(score + delta, 100)` — capped, never negative, never decrements mid-session — and a bucket update that only fires if the new bucket **outranks** the current one on the fixed priority list `[emergency, active, comparison, research, unclassified]`. Denise starts at `unclassified` (rank 4, the lowest), so the very first event — `page_load`, tagged `research` (rank 3) — immediately outranks it and sets her bucket. After that, every other `research`-tagged event on this page just adds points; it doesn't need to fight for the bucket again, it already won. If she'd scrolled to 75%, `scroll_75` fires tagged `comparison` (rank 2) — outranking research — and her bucket would escalate mid-visit, no matter what page she's on. She didn't get that far this time. Score 15, bucket Research — which, separately, is also guaranteed by a simpler rule: landing on `/blog` at all is one of the three conditions that satisfies Research on its own (§3.6 `isResearchBucket()`), no minimum score required.*

*→ So by the time she closes the tab: one Tier 2B identity record (cookie + fingerprint, no name), sitting at Engagement Score 15, bucket Research. Two numbers, two purposes — the tier says "we don't know who this is yet, don't act on it," the score/bucket says "here's what she's actually doing, keep adapting the content to it regardless of whether we know her name."*

**Three days later**, sitting at her kitchen table, she opens her laptop and goes back to RightFlush's site to look a bit more — this time the before/after gallery. She scrolls about halfway through the photos, maybe 35 seconds, then closes it.

*→ Identity side, same sequence, independently: validity passes, she clears 10 seconds, the system checks the DB for the laptop's fingerprint before creating anything. Here's the wrinkle worth sitting with — no match. Her laptop is a completely different device, so it looks like a completely different person as far as the fingerprint check is concerned. A second Tier 2B record gets created, cookie and fingerprint only, same as the first. **As far as the system is concerned right now, these are two unconnected Tier 2B individuals** — not one woman on two devices. This is exactly the scenario the cross-device fingerprint cap exists for (`ClearSky_Serhii_Developer_Brief`: *cannot claim 70%+ from device signals alone when switching iOS Safari to Desktop Chrome*): even if a fingerprint match fires between these two records later, it's structurally capped below 70% confidence — never enough, on its own, to merge them. Honest signals, not an edge case someone forgot to handle.*

*→ Intent side — and here's what's genuinely different this time, off the gallery page's real numbers:*

| Event | Delta | Bucket tag | Running score |
|---|---|---|---|
| `page_load` | +5 | comparison | **5** |
| `scroll_25` | +3 | comparison | **8** |
| `scroll_50` | +5 | comparison | **13** |
| `dwell_30` | +4 | comparison | **17** |

*→ Her bucket starts fresh at `unclassified` again — brand-new device, brand-new client-side state, nothing carries over from the phone. `page_load` on the gallery page is tagged `comparison` (rank 2), which immediately outranks `unclassified` and sets the bucket. Every event after it — `scroll_25`, `scroll_50`, `dwell_30` — carries the page's own bucket (`PAGE_BUCKET`), which is `comparison` here, so they all reinforce it; nothing has to fight for the bucket, it was set on load. **This session lands on Comparison, score 17** — a different bucket than the phone session's Research, even though it's the same real person doing what is, in plain language, the same thing: looking at what RightFlush can do for a bathroom.*

Two Tier 2B records now exist, invisible to each other — one Research/15, one Comparison/17 — both slowly building a picture of a woman who hasn't decided anything yet — she's just looking.

*→ One honest caveat on all those numbers: `+3` for a page load, `+4` for thirty seconds of dwell, "≥2 signals" to earn Active Project — none of that was derived from watching real people convert. It's deterministic by design at launch, not by conviction, for the exact same reason the Orchestrator starts with a fixed rules-based decision table instead of a predictive model (§10, `clearsky-master-architecture.md`): there's no Cohort 2 conversion data yet to calibrate against. Once enough licensed clients accumulate enough booked jobs, these deltas and thresholds get recalibrated against what actually predicted a conversion, not what seemed reasonable at design time. The four buckets and the 0–100 score aren't going anywhere — the numbers underneath them are a first draft, not a finding.*

## Chapter 2 — The Record That Couldn't Survive the Silence

Denise doesn't touch RightFlush's site again for over a week. Life happens — work, her kid's hockey schedule, the bathroom stays annoying but not urgent. Both records just sit there, untouched.

*→ Two separate clocks are now running, and they are not the same clock. Per `ClearSky_Section5_Four_Intent_Buckets_Report__1_.md`, demotion only fires when two conditions are met **simultaneously**: `score_live` (a decayed read of the frozen `score_raw`) falls below the bucket's threshold, *and* idle time exceeds that bucket's grace period. Neither condition alone is enough — the system is deliberately built not to punish someone for a normal gap between sessions.*

*→ **The laptop record (Comparison, score_raw 17, grace 7 days, half-life 30 days).** Here's the part worth being honest about — it's not really a decay story. The Comparison demotion threshold is `score_live < 20`. Her `score_raw` was already 17 the moment she closed the laptop on day 3 — already under the threshold, before a single hour of decay had even happened. Condition 1 was true from the start. All that was ever standing between this record and demotion was the 7-day grace period itself. By day 11 — eight days idle — Condition 2 is met too. Both conditions true simultaneously: **the laptop record demotes from Comparison to Research** on the next profile read. One modest 35-second visit was enough to earn a bucket, but not enough to survive a week and a half of silence in it.*

*→ **The phone record (Research, score_raw 15, grace 14 days, half-life 60 days).** At day 11 she's not even at the grace boundary yet — 11 days idle against a 14-day grace. Condition 2 isn't even met. It doesn't matter that Research's threshold (`score_live < 8`) is much lower and harder to fall under anyway — this record was never close to being at risk. It's the lower-intent bucket of the two, and it's also the one that survives.*

On day 11, half-watching TV again, Denise opens her phone — not the laptop — and navigates straight back to RightFlush's site.

*→ Before a word of content even renders, here's what's real. Her `score_raw` never decayed — it's frozen at 15 from the blog visit and simply resumes. Nothing multiplies: the engagement score is flat point deltas capped at 100 (`firePixel()` runs `score = min(score + delta, 100)`). There is one return-visit **score** adjustment — a **recency bonus: a recognized return within 3 days adds a flat +10** on top of the page_load (design decision, July 2026 — additive, not a multiplier, and a first-draft number to calibrate against Cohort 2 like every other delta). Denise's return is on **day 11**, so it doesn't apply here — had she come back within three days, she'd have picked up the extra 10. Separately, the plumbing rules config carries a *content* rule (not a score one): on a visitor's 3rd return it reassigns Research to Comparison — also not firing yet, this being only her second visit on this phone. Coming back after eleven days keeps her the same tracked 2B record, score resuming exactly where it left off.*

*→ Worth being exact about what she is at this exact moment: still **Tier 2B.** Nothing about returning or reading identifies her — no email yet, no name, just the same cookie and fingerprint from day 0. Her `score_raw` picks up where it left off: 15 (frozen from the blog visit) + 4 (FAQ `page_load`) = 19 before she's scrolled at all.*

She clicks into the FAQ. Reads a couple of answers, scrolling as she goes — `scroll_25` (+3), `scroll_50` (+5), `dwell_30` (+4) — then keeps reading past a minute, firing `dwell_60` (+7). Running total: 19 + 3 + 5 + 4 + 7 = **38.** All of it tagged `research` (the FAQ page's bucket); she never reaches `scroll_75`, so nothing escalates her.

*→ On the page sits a dynamic content container — not static HTML, a slot the push engine fills per visitor. This is Layer 4's Site Personalization output, and here's the piece that matters for a Tier 2B visitor specifically: it isn't gated on identity at all. **Tier 1 gets it unconditionally; Tier 2 and 2B get it once score crosses 35** — a threshold generalized from an existing example in the Orchestrator's decision table (§10), not invented for this scene. She just crossed it. No email required, no channel needed, because Site Personalization was never a channel-based action — it's changing what she sees on a page she's already looking at, not reaching out to her. The push engine reads bucket and score, and selects a pre-approved content variant she hasn't already been shown this session — falling back to the bucket's default if she'd already seen everything eligible. Nothing here is generated on the spot.*

*→ The Orchestrator has real signal to work with: she came back after eleven days (persistence), she's now on her second page ever on this device (blog, then FAQ — a pattern, not a bounce), and she's reading, not skimming. But she's still Research bucket, and Research's push rules (`clearsky-ma-rules-config-plumbing.md`) are explicit: **no quote form, suppress pricing and booking proof.** So the manifest doesn't reach for a discount banner or a booking CTA — that's a Comparison/Active Project move, not this one, and would be off-limits regardless of score since she's nowhere near Tier 1 yet. It reaches for exactly the thing built for this bucket and this visit-two, content-already-consumed condition: the lead-magnet guide. Soft, not pushy, aimed at getting her attention without asking for anything but an email.*

The banner renders: *"Not sure what you need? Download our free Homeowner's Guide to Bathroom Renovation in Timmins."* No obligation, no quote form, just a guide. She types her email in and hits download.

*→ This is the moment everything from earlier in the day collapses into one identity. It's an `email_capture` conversion — `conversionType: email_capture`, only `emailHash` populated, no phone, no name. `GET /hub/profiles/resolve` finds no existing match. `POST /hub/profiles/merge` runs with `profileId: null` and her phone's `anonymousId` — the same persistent cookie that's been sitting on this device since day 0 — so it folds in **all** of her phone history, the blog visit included, not just today. Per the Tier 1 definition in `clearsky-master-architecture` — *strong identifier resolves cleanly: phone, email hash, or forwarded token* — an email hash alone is a strong identifier. **She's Tier 1, immediately** — full pipeline, full profile, same tier a completed quote form would have earned her, off nothing but an email address on a guide download. Bucket stays Research; downloading a guide isn't a stronger behavioral signal than reading a blog post was.*

*→ She also qualifies for NurtureSequence on the spot — Tier 1, bucket Research, exactly the trigger condition in Component 6 of the hub integration spec. No one calls her. No one texts her. Once a month, a plain, useful email shows up — the same tone as the guide she just downloaded, not a sales pitch — until her bucket moves or she converts or she unsubscribes.*

*→ And the laptop record — still sitting there, demoted to Research, cookie and fingerprint only, no name attached — doesn't get any of this. Nothing about today's email capture reaches back and merges it. That's not a bug the team forgot to close; it's the cross-device fingerprint cap (`ClearSky_Serhii_Developer_Brief`) doing exactly what it's supposed to do. Even if a fingerprint match were run right now between her phone and her laptop, it's structurally capped below 70% — never enough, on its own, to claim they're the same device without a matching conversion event happening on the laptop itself. As far as the system honestly knows, Denise-the-named-contact and whoever-browsed-the-gallery-on-a-laptop are still two different people. One of them just isn't anonymous anymore.*

## Chapter 3 — The Email, the Return, and the Call

A month goes by. On schedule — `nextSendAt` from the record created the moment she downloaded the guide — an email lands in Denise's inbox. Plain subject line, no pressure: a couple of paragraphs on what actually drives bathroom renovation costs in Timmins, tips she can use whether or not she ever calls RightFlush.

*→ Worth being honest about what this email is and isn't. It's the default `plumbing_monthly_tips` template, Automate mode, no human touching it before it sends — the same tone Research bucket always gets (educational, no obligation), not something written specifically about *her* bathroom project. The idea that it should sound like a natural continuation of the guide she downloaded — bathroom-reno tips specifically, not generic plumbing tips — is a real direction the team has talked about but hasn't specced or built. Right now she gets the same email every Research-bucket Tier-1 contact gets.*

The email carries a link back to the site — signed, `purpose: follow_up`, same `cs_token` mechanism as any forwarded link. She doesn't click it. A month later a second one goes out; no click on that one either.

*→ Here's the honest limit of what the system knows: nothing about whether she read those first two emails. ClearSky tracks **clicks, not opens** — by design, not omission. Open-tracking relies on a pixel that Apple's Mail Privacy Protection auto-fires for roughly half of all recipients whether they look or not, so an "open" is false signal for the majority. A click can't lie that way. So emails one and two are genuine silence: she may have read both and not been ready, or deleted them unread — the system can't tell, and doesn't pretend to. Her bucket, her score, her tier: all unchanged. All it knows is she hasn't clicked yet.*

On the third one, she clicks the link — mid-afternoon, at work, thinking about it again after her sister mentions a contractor she used. *This* is the first real signal since the guide download: a `cs_token` follow-up click, tied to a specific email, unambiguous. Not an open the system half-guessed at — a deliberate return she chose.

*→ Doesn't matter that the link carries a `cs_token` — her phone already has something better. After the merge back in Chapter 2, Component 5 step 5 retired the old `anonymousId` cookie and replaced it with one carrying her `hubProfileId` directly. This phone doesn't need the token to know who she is anymore; it already knows. Named from the first pageview, same as any forwarded link, just for a quieter reason — this is simply her device now.*

She lands on the Bathroom Renovations page — the one page from Chapter 1 she read about but never actually visited. Reads the whole thing. Looks at the before/after gallery embedded halfway down. Then clicks the page's **Call Now** button.

*→ `call_click` fires — **+15, tagged `active`** (the same locked delta any phone-number click gets, per the Comprehensive Platform Report's scoring table), a strong signal by design. Her bucket has been sitting at Research this whole time; `active` outranks it on the priority list, same escalate-only mechanism from Chapter 1, and it flips the instant the event fires. No ambiguity about who's calling, either — this isn't a cold inbound call needing caller-ID lookup against the CRM. The call originates from a session that already knows her hubProfileId. A2P doesn't have to guess.*

The rep picks up already looking at her file: the blog post from four months ago, the guide download, three nurture emails sent with the third one clicked, today's visit to the Bathroom Renovations page. Not a cold call on either end.

*→ Worth pausing on how the call actually found her file, because it reveals the architecture. When she tapped Call Now, the Orchestrator didn't hand her RightFlush's main line — it pulled a **Telnyx tracking number** from the pool and bound it to her `sessionId` for a short window. She dialed that number; the inbound call arrived on it; the system looked up which session that number was bound to and landed the call at RightFlush already showing her profile. The part that matters: **her caller ID was never the key — the number she dialed was.** Which means this works identically if the caller is still **2B.** Swap Denise-the-named-contact for an anonymous visitor who's never given a name: same tap, same tracking-number bind, and the call still lands with her full history on the rep's screen — returning visitor, Research bucket, viewed the blog and the FAQ, no name yet. The binding carries whatever the session knows; being anonymous doesn't break it. The one thing that breaks it is time: the number is bound for a TTL of minutes, not hours — dial it after that window has released and reassigned it, and she lands cold, an ordinary inbound call matched only on caller ID. (Full flow: `clearsky-call-binding-spec`.)* *And the same key works off your own turf. Denise tapped a button on a page you control, so she got a **session-bound** number — dynamic, pulled from a pool, carrying her whole live session. But most calls in the wild don't start on your site: someone taps Call straight from your Google listing, an ad, a directory, a QR code on the truck. Those surfaces have no session and no pixel — so each gets its own **dedicated, permanent** number instead. A call on the GBP number is known to be a GBP call the instant it rings; identity is then whatever a caller-ID match can confirm. Same Telnyx capability, two assignment models: dynamic-and-session-bound for owned surfaces, static-and-source-bound for everywhere else — and the off-platform numbers double as the cleanest attribution signal ClearSky gets, because the number itself says where the call came from.*

*→ Before the rep ever picks up, a few more things are already true. The IVR plays its required consent notice — *"this call may be recorded"* — then routes on a single keypress: Denise presses 2 for Estimates/Quotes, the same two-menu-depth, DTMF-driven routing the A2P spec locks down (`ClearSky_A2P_Developer_Spec.md` §3). That selection is logged as `ivr_path: main_menu > estimates` on the call event record the instant she presses it, appended at each step rather than only at the end. Recording begins automatically the moment the call connects and runs until she hangs up.*

*→ Her keypress itself is worth a point value — and it's worth being precise about where that value actually gets decided. The IVR's job is routing only; the A2P spec is explicit that it "does not make business decisions" (§3.1), so pressing 2 doesn't score anything inside the IVR itself. What happens instead: the IVR fires a webhook — `call.dtmf.received`, then `call.gather.ended` once the selection is confirmed, both already in the A2P spec's own webhook table (§8) — and Event Intake reacts to it the same way it reacts to any other provider event, applying a flat, deterministic delta off which menu option she picked. Same table-lookup mechanism as `page_load`/`scroll_25` on the web side, just triggered by a different provider:*

| IVR Selection | Delta | Tag |
|---|---|---|
| 1 — Service/Repairs | +6 | active |
| 2 — Estimates/Quotes | +10 | active |
| 3 — Emergency | routes through the existing locked +20 Emergency override, not a separate delta | emergency |
| 4 — Billing/Admin | +2 | unchanged |
| 0 — Operator / no input | +0 | unchanged |

*Denise presses 2. **+10, active** — on top of the +15 from `call_click`, before the call has even connected to a rep. One honest gap, same family as the CallEvent bridge flagged below: nothing in any spec read for this document shows Event Intake actually listening for `call.dtmf.received` and applying a delta. The table above is the mechanism this scene needs, not one already built — it's the same unbuilt bridge, just needing to fire mid-call instead of only once at the end.*

*→ A `call_event_id` gets minted the moment Telnyx's `call.initiated` webhook fires — before the IVR menu even plays. This is the first row in what the schema's `network_categories` table calls her **Communication** category (inbound/outbound voice, SMS, and email activity). Worth being precise about what that means: RightFlush has technically "communicated" with Denise before — a guide and two nurture emails went to her inbox — but those were automated one-way sends, nobody on the other end reading or responding in real time. This is the first time a live person is about to talk to her directly. The `call_event_id` this call gets is a genuinely new identifier, not a continuation of anything; her session, fingerprint, and `hubProfileId` carry forward from Chapter 1, but there's no prior call_event on file to link this one to. (One naming wrinkle worth a flag: the A2P spec's own example writes this ID as `evt_<uuid>` — the same prefix the AI Decision System uses for its pipeline event_id elsewhere in these specs. Whether that means the same events table, or two similarly-named but separate ones, isn't resolved in what's been read for this document.)*

*→ So by the time the rep picks up, her Engagement Score already reflects two real, deterministic events tonight — `call_click` (+15) and the IVR selection (+10) — the same flat, additive, never-decrements mechanism as every other delta in this document, just triggered off a phone event instead of a page event. What happens on the call itself works the same way, just resolved later: if it ends in a booking, that fires the same `conversionType: booking` / "Appointment booked" event the web funnel already has locked at **+20** — the single highest delta in the system, whether she'd submitted a form or closed it live on the phone. If the call goes badly instead — a complaint, a churn-risk conversation — the score doesn't drop; it can't, the Engagement Score has no decrement mechanism, by design. But it also doesn't get rewarded the way a booking does; nothing here scores a bad outcome as if it were a good one. The bad outcome gets captured on a different axis entirely — Signal Detection's `COMPLAINT_SIGNAL`/`CHURN_RISK` classification (§6.4), not a penalty on this score. How engaged she was and whether this went well for the business stay two separate questions, the same way identity and intent stayed separate back in Chapter 1.*

*→ This is where the whole loop most of this document has been building toward finally closes — though worth being precise about which system does what here. Connecting the call to a rep with her file already up isn't the Orchestrator making a decision; it's the faster, simpler layer underneath it — IVR routing plus the profile lookup the call-binding spec describes, required to resolve in under two seconds so it never holds up the greeting (`ClearSky_A2P_Developer_Spec.md` §2.2). The Orchestrator's actual job — Signal Detection, ranking, deciding what to do next — hasn't run yet. It only sees this call once the recording is transcribed, after she hangs up. Her bucket is **Active Project** regardless, the same escalation `call_click` triggered a moment ago — not a separate "Opportunity" tier, just the top rung of the same four-bucket ladder Chapter 1 introduced.*

## Chapter 4 — The Booking, the Outcome, and the Loop That Closes

Denise explains the bathroom — the grout, the tub, a full renovation this time, not just a repair. The rep, holding her whole trajectory in one screen, doesn't have to reconstruct any of it: how she found RightFlush, what she's already read, what she was interested enough in to leave an email for but not a phone number, until today. A full reno isn't something he'll quote blind over the phone — he walks her through the guarantee she never actually visited on-site but the guide had already mentioned, and offers an in-home consultation instead: someone comes out, looks at the space, puts a real number on it. She agrees. They land on a time together — **July 20th, 10:00 AM, at her place** — and both repeat it back before they hang up. Before that, the rep asks one more thing: is a text reminder okay, or email only? She says text is fine.

*→ She hangs up. Only now does the rest of the machinery start. `call.hangup` fires, the recording finalizes, and — per the same Live Call Handling flow voicemails use (`ClearSky_A2P_Developer_Spec.md` §6.6) — the full call goes to Deepgram for transcription, then to the AI Analysis Engine. The engine scores the transcript across its six locked dimensions: sentiment *positive*, urgency *medium* (not urgent — a planned reno, not a burst pipe), emergency *false*, opportunity *high*, momentum `ready_to_book` — she called intending to move forward and booked a consultation, which is exactly what this dimension is measuring: her disposition during the conversation, not a record of whether the job itself ever sells. Whether the renovation gets sold is a separate, later fact — this call only committed to a visit. The free-text summary carries the part Momentum doesn't: *"Homeowner booked an in-home consultation for a full bathroom renovation — July 20th, 10:00 AM, at her residence. Mentioned wanting a heated towel rack added if it fits the budget — flag for the estimate. No urgency; standard scheduling."* That second sentence is the part worth noticing — not the appointment itself, just something she mentioned in passing that a transcript catches and a busy rep might not write down.*

*→ Here's the piece that has to exist for any of this to actually reach Section 6/7 — and it's worth being upfront that it doesn't yet, not in any spec read for this document. Right now Denise's call lives as a `CallEvent` row: `call_id`, `ivr_path`, `recording_url`, `transcript_id`, an `id` of its own. But `CallEvent` has no `event_id` field, and the `events` table — the one Signal Detection, the Orchestrator, Outcome, and Feedback all actually key off — has no column pointing back to a `CallEvent` either. Nothing bridges them today, and — as Chapter 3's IVR selection made clear — this bridge actually needs to fire **twice**, not once: once at `call.dtmf.received`/`call.gather.ended` mid-call, so the IVR-selection delta has an `events` row to attach to, and again here at call completion, so the AI Analysis output does. The mechanism this scene needs: Event Intake creates a new `events` row at each of those points — `provider: 'telnyx_voice'`, `provider_event_id: <call_id>`, `event_type` set to whatever stage fired (`call_dtmf_received` or `call_completed`), `network_category: 'Communication'` (all already registered in the `provider_event_registry`, §1 of `002_seed_rules.sql`) — and writes whatever's available at that stage (the IVR selection, or later the full AI analysis output: sentiment, momentum, summary, `signals_detected`) into that row's `normalized_payload`, the same generic JSONB column GBP data presumably passed through before it earned its own dedicated `gbp_review_id`/`rating`/`review_text` columns. Two small schema additions would close the loop cleanly: a `call_event_id` column on `events` (mirroring how GBP got dedicated fields), and an `ai_analysis_id` column on `CallEvent` itself — `Voicemail` already has that field, `CallEvent` doesn't. **None of this exists yet. It needs to be built before a live call can actually reach Section 6/7 the way this chapter describes** — and it needs to be built as a multi-fire mechanism, not a single event-completion hook. Everything from here on — the `event_id` Signal Detection reports against, the one Outcome and Feedback link back to — assumes this bridging row already got created.*

*→ The Orchestrator receives this as a `live_call_analysis_package` and runs Signal Detection on this call for the first time. The match in the locked Signal table (§6.4) is `BOOKING_OPPORTUNITY` (momentum = ready_to_book) — a clean fit here, not a stretch. That maps to two Actions, both automatic, neither requiring approval: `ACT-CALL-004` (log the opportunity — and the scheduled consultation — to CRM) and `ACT-CALL-001` (create a task for the towel-rack mention, so it doesn't just sit in a transcript nobody rereads). Both execute immediately; neither sits in a queue waiting on Sarah.*

*→ One more thing gets scheduled off this same call: a reminder for July 20th, both channels — email and SMS. Both need to carry the same reference back to this call rather than reading as two isolated messages, and the schema already has the field built for exactly that: `thread_id`, a relationships column on `events` (`001_initial_schema.sql`), set here to this call's own `CallEvent.id` — the id already minted at `call.initiated`, the one this story's been calling her "comm id." The topic isn't "Denise" generically, it's specifically *the July 20th consultation*; the call, the reminder email, and the reminder SMS are three different channels describing the same one thing, tied together by reusing one id rather than minting a new one per message.*

*→ The email side is well-trodden ground. A signed link, `purpose: appointment_reminder` — already a locked value in the `cs_token` purpose enum (`ClearSky_MA_Requirements_v3.md`; `clearsky-ma-hub-integration-spec.md` §2), sitting right alongside `follow_up` and `quote_ready`. It gets scheduled the same way a NurtureSequence email does — a `nextSendAt` field, set here to the day before, July 19th, rather than the monthly cadence Research-bucket nurture uses.*

*→ The SMS side now has somewhere structured for that consent to live: `CallEvent.sms_consent`, a new field, nullable — null meaning it never came up on the call. It isn't the IVR asserting it, and it isn't the rep typing it into a CRM field by hand; the AI Analysis Engine populates it the same pass it already reads the transcript for sentiment and momentum, listening for exactly the exchange that happened here — the rep asking, Denise answering yes. That's the consent the only SMS Action in the A2P spec's library, `ACT-CALL-008` — "Send SMS follow-up (if opted in)" — actually needs before it fires; the "(if opted in)" qualifier in its own name is satisfied here, not skipped, and now has a real field backing it instead of nothing. One piece still open, unchanged by adding the field: `ACT-CALL-008` still carries `Requires Approval: Yes` regardless of what `sms_consent` says — a sensible default for a marketing follow-up text, still a strange fit for a routine "see you tomorrow at 10" confirmation that shouldn't need Sarah's sign-off any more than `ACT-CALL-004` logging a CRM note does. Consent has a home now; the approval gate still doesn't know that.*

*→ Worth pausing on what just happened, mechanically, because two systems are now describing the same appointment from two different angles. The pixel/hub layer fires `conversionType: booking` — locked alongside `call_click` and `email_capture` (`clearsky-ma-hub-integration-spec.md` Component 1) — against her `hubProfileId`, applying the same **+20 "Appointment booked"** delta the story flagged back in Chapter 3, the highest single delta the score system has. Worth being precise about what actually got booked: not the renovation itself — the July 20th consultation. Separately, the A2P/Orchestrator pipeline just logged its own `ACT-CALL-004` execution recording the same scheduled visit, off the call transcript rather than the web event. Nothing in the specs read for this document says whether these two are meant to reconcile into one record or simply coexist as two views of the same fact — worth flagging for whoever owns both integrations, not resolving here. What follows applies Section 6/7's Outcome and Feedback machinery to the A2P side specifically, since that's the pipeline with actual Execution records to close out.*

*→ One honest note before going further: the developer reference for this machinery (`ClearSky_Section6_Outcome_Developer_Reference.md`, `ClearSky_Section7_Feedback_Full_Documentation.md`) was built and demonstrated against one concrete scenario — APEX Contracting's 1-star review needing a drafted reply — not a phone booking. That's a prototype choice, not a scope limit: the schema and eligibility rules key off `execution_id`, `action_queue_id`, `event_id`, `decision_id`, and a generic `execution_status` enum, none of it review-specific, and both this document and the A2P spec quote the identical locked workflow (`Event → Signal Detection → Orchestrator Decision → Action Queue + Parameters → Execution → Outcome → Feedback`) as the one pipeline every channel feeds into. So what follows is the same generic engine applied to a call instead of a review, not a new one improvised for the occasion.*

*→ **Outcome (Section 6).** Both executions — `ACT-CALL-004` and `ACT-CALL-001` — are automatic, no draft, no approval gate, nothing waiting on Sarah Jenkins. That maps each to `outcome_type: completed`, `outcome_status: completed`, the same landing spot any fully-automatic internal action gets. `posted_externally` stays **FALSE** on both — nothing went out to Google, a review site, or any public channel; this was a private call and an internal CRM log. `approval_wait_hours` is **NULL** on both — there was never anything to wait on. Each record links back to the same `event_id` — the bridging row Event Intake just created — and the Signal Detection decision from a moment ago, so a support person auditing Denise's file later sees one unbroken chain from *hung up the phone* to *two logged outcomes*, not fragments. (No worked example anywhere in the specs shows an `ACT-CALL-*` ID actually landing in the `outcomes` table the way `ACT-REV-001` does — but that's because nobody's published one yet, not because the mechanism doesn't fit; the same `action_queue_id`/`execution_id` foreign keys Section 6 requires are exactly what the Orchestrator's Action Queue step is supposed to populate regardless of which Action Library produced the record.)*

*→ **Feedback (Section 7).** Once Outcome closes on both records, Feedback evaluates what they actually showed — a readback, not a guess. `signal_validity: likely_valid` on both — about as validated as a signal gets; `BOOKING_OPPORTUNITY` called it correctly, and the consultation she booked confirms it rather than complicating it. `action_execution_quality: worked_as_expected` on both — the execution paths did exactly what they're built to do. `human_review_state: not_applicable` on both — there was no draft sitting in `waiting_for_human_approval`; nothing needed Sarah's sign-off, so the field is never bent into an "approved" state it never actually passed through. `production_rules_changed: FALSE`, same as always — two logged actions don't rewrite a threshold or a template on their own. One schema gap worth flagging rather than papering over: `decision_quality`'s only positive value in the current schema is `reasonable_so_far` — the "so_far" suffix is documented as meaning the outcome is still partial, but nothing in the schema offers a plain "reasonable" state for a fully completed one like this. It gets `reasonable_so_far` anyway, because that's the only non-negative value that exists — not because either outcome is actually still pending.*

*→ **Tuning candidates — candidate_only, never automatic.** This is where Denise's specific trajectory earns its keep for the next visitor, without touching anything live tonight: a four-month, three-email, one-call conversion is exactly the kind of data point the "first draft, not a finding" caveat from Chapter 1 was waiting for. Candidates this record could reasonably surface — all `automatic_change: false`, all requiring a person to review before anything changes — include whether the +10 recency bonus and the Research/Comparison grace periods held up against a real booking instead of a guess, whether three nurture touches is close to the right number before a Research-bucket Tier-1 contact converts, and whether the guide-download → FAQ → nurture → call sequence that worked for her shows up often enough across Cohort 2 to promote from "this is what happened once" to "this is the expected path." None of that changes tonight. It sits exactly where Section 7 requires it to sit — a candidate, not a commit.*

*→ One more thing changes the moment this appointment gets booked, worth stating here rather than leaving Chapter 5 to explain it after the fact: bucket demotion — the `score_live`-decay-plus-grace-period mechanism from Chapter 2 — gets suspended on her profile for as long as `appointment_id` stays open and future-dated. That mechanism measures pre-conversion interest decay: is someone still worth nurturing, or has their attention genuinely lapsed? Denise isn't in that funnel anymore — she's mid-transaction, with a real date on the calendar. Demoting her to Comparison because a few idle days passed before July 20th would be measuring the wrong thing entirely; the demotion math was never built to represent "already converted, waiting on an appointment." The suspension lifts the moment the appointment closes — completed, cancelled, or no-showed — and the ordinary decay clock resumes from wherever her score actually sits then.*

The Cohort 2 network gets one more real trajectory: a Research-bucket, Tier-1, email-only contact who took four months, three nurture touches, and one clicked link to convert — and now, a scheduled in-home consultation on top of it. The job itself isn't sold yet; that's July 20th's problem, not tonight's. Nobody re-runs Denise's scoring, her tier, or her bucket history to make the story cleaner in hindsight; Feedback records what happened, not what should have happened. The next Denise-shaped visitor the Orchestrator sees inherits a slightly better-calibrated system, one real appointment at a time.

## Chapter 5 — The Voicemail

Four days before the consultation, Tuesday afternoon, Denise remembers a conflict — her sister needs a ride somewhere that same morning. She calls RightFlush back.

*→ The number she has is the one from her call log — the same session-bound tracking number the Orchestrator handed her on Call Now, days ago. That binding is long gone; a TTL of minutes doesn't survive four days. Per the call-binding spec's own fallback table, dialing it now means no session to resolve — this call lands cold, an ordinary inbound call, matched only on caller ID. But "cold" doesn't mean anonymous: the A2P spec's profile lookup (§2.2) runs on every inbound call regardless of which number was dialed, checking caller ID against known profiles — and her caller ID has been on file since the very first `CallEvent` row, back when she tapped Call Now. It resolves to her profile the ordinary way, just without the rich live-session handoff the session-bound number gave the rep last time.*

*→ One honest flag here, not resolved: whether "ClearSky CRM" profile matching (§7.1, `caller_id` → `profile.phone`) and the marketing-automation hub's `hubProfileId` are the same profile record, or two separate ones that each happen to know her phone number, isn't shown in any spec read for this document. If they're separate, this call resolves her identity on the A2P side without necessarily touching the same profile the web pixel and NurtureSequence write to — worth confirming, not assumed either way here.*

She goes through the same IVR, presses 2 for Estimates/Quotes — same **+10, active** delta from the table in Chapter 3, firing again. Worth naming plainly rather than engineering around: that's noise, not signal. Denise already converted; nothing about pressing 2 a second time moves her meaningfully closer to anything. But the delta table was never built to distinguish "new interest" from "an existing customer calling back," and it doesn't need to — Event Intake's job is to normalize and match, not to judge whether a real event was worth scoring. The number moves, harmlessly, and nothing downstream mistakes it for a fresh opportunity, because that judgment happens one layer later, correctly, in Signal Detection. It's doubly harmless here, in fact: the demotion suspension that started the moment she booked the consultation (Chapter 4) means her bucket isn't at risk from this call either way, whether the score moves or not.

*→ Twenty seconds pass with no pickup. Per the A2P spec's voicemail trigger table (§4.2), an unanswered call past 20 seconds transfers automatically to the voicemail flow. The greeting plays its required line — *"your message will be reviewed and you'll receive a callback"* — no promised callback time, per the spec's own instruction not to invent one.*

She leaves a message: *"Hi, it's Denise — I've got the consultation booked for July 20th at 10, but something came up that morning. Any chance we could move it to noon instead? Give me a call back when you can. Thanks."*

*→ The Voicemail Processing Pipeline runs exactly as documented (§4.3): recorded by Telnyx, uploaded to ClearSky storage, transcribed, the transcript stored, then handed to the AI Analysis Engine. A `Voicemail` record gets created — `voicemail_id`, `call_event_id`, `profile_id`, `recording_url`, `transcript_id`, `transcript_text`, `duration_seconds`, and eventually `ai_analysis_id` — the same field this document already flagged as missing from `CallEvent`. `Voicemail` has always had it.*

*→ AI Analysis reads the transcript: sentiment *neutral* — she's not upset, just rearranging a morning. Urgency *medium* — there's a real deadline (July 20th), not an emergency. Emergency *false*. Opportunity — here's a real gap worth naming rather than forcing an answer: nothing this call represents is a *new* opportunity, it's an existing booked customer asking to move an existing appointment, and the dimension's own definition — "whether a revenue or growth opportunity is present" — doesn't have a clean answer for that case. It gets logged `none`, defensible but not quite right either. Momentum has the same problem, more sharply: `passive / interested / ready_to_book` has no value for "already a customer, just adjusting logistics." It gets whichever's closest — `interested`, probably — but this is the dimension table actually missing a real case, not narrative color stretched to fit the way `ready_to_book` turned out to genuinely fit the booking call.*

*→ Signal Detection has a cleaner answer, for once: `CALLBACK_REQUIRED` — `call_outcome = voicemail`, `urgency ≥ medium` — a direct table match, no stretch needed (§6.4). That maps to `ACT-CALL-001`, automatic, no approval: a new callback task, a second instance of the same Action that logged the towel-rack note back in Chapter 4 — one Action ID, reused for a different specific ask, exactly how an Action Library is supposed to work. The task itself: call Denise back about moving July 20th to noon. Nothing about the appointment changes yet — nobody's confirmed noon works on RightFlush's end, and this system doesn't invent a confirmation that hasn't happened. That's a callback still waiting to happen, not a done thing.*

*→ Here's where the mechanism from Chapter 4 needs a real step added, not just an assumption. Left to the default — a fresh `CallEvent`, a fresh bridging `events` row — this call mints its own new `thread_id`, unrelated to the July 20th consultation, purely because nothing yet checks whether it should. That default is wrong for this call, and the system has to actually catch it, not just narrate the right answer after the fact. This splits across two stages, not one — worth being precise about which does what, because it's a real, existing pattern in this system, not a new one invented for this scene.*

*→ **Event Intake (S1)** does the scoring. Its "AI extract" job (the S1-S7 table's own words: "Normalize, enrich, match, AI extract") compares this call against the profile's recent history — same caller_id, an open `appointment_id`, and the transcript's own content ("the consultation," "July 20th," "move it") against that appointment's details — and writes a confidence value into `ai_context.thread_match_confidence`. That's a real field this document is proposing, but the *shape* of it isn't new: `ai_context.confidence_score` already drives several of the 40 signal rules (`SIG-CONV-002`, `SIG-COM-003`, others), so this is the same mechanism, pointed at a different question.*

*→ **Signal Detection (S2)** does the threshold check — not Event Intake, which only produces the number, never decides what it means. A new rule, `SIG-COM-006` (`thread_continuation_detected`), evaluates `ai_context.thread_match_confidence >= 0.75` — the same condition shape as `SIG-CONV-002`'s `ai_context.confidence_score >= 0.80`, just a new rule for a new field. Denise's case clears it easily: same caller, an open appointment on the exact topic named in the transcript, four days out. When it fires, `thread_id` reconciles to the original rather than staying fresh and disconnected, and the signal maps to `ACT-NOTIFY-001` — an existing Action, not a new one, already built to notify "the business owner or assigned employee" and already carrying `consultant_id` as an optional parameter. Sarah gets notified that the merge happened, in the communication log; she doesn't have to approve it, the reconciliation already ran. But she can see that it happened and why, which is the actual point of flagging instead of hiding it.*

## Chapter 6 — The Reschedule

No one calls Denise back. `ACT-CALL-001`'s task resolves without a live conversation — there's nothing left to ask her, she already said exactly what she wants in the voicemail. What the task actually triggers is an availability check, and the AI Analysis Engine queries RightFlush's appointment schedule directly: is noon open on July 20th for whoever's assigned to this consultation? It comes back confirmed — noon is open.

*→ Worth being honest about what's real here and what isn't. The check itself — AI querying a schedule, getting a yes or no back — is a clean, deterministic pattern, the same shape as every other AI-driven confirmation in this document (the AI Analysis Engine's sentiment/momentum reads, `sms_consent`). What isn't real: there's no spec anywhere — not the A2P spec, not the AI Decision System schema, nowhere else read for this project — describing an actual appointment or technician-scheduling system for the AI to query. `appointment_id` exists as a relationship field on `events`; nothing describes a calendar, a booking table, or an availability API sitting behind it. This scene assumes one exists and returns "yes, noon is open" — a placeholder for a system that needs to be designed and built, not a locked mechanism.*

*→ With availability confirmed, the appointment record itself changes — same `appointment_id`, not a new one. The 10:00 AM slot releases; the same appointment re-books at 12:00 PM. Same gap as the availability check above, not a separate one: whatever holds "released" and "booked" states for a time slot doesn't exist in any spec read for this project either.*

Two things follow immediately, both grounded in mechanisms this document already has rather than invented fresh: the reminder, and a confirmation back to Denise.

*→ **The reminder updates.** Chapter 4's reminder — `purpose: appointment_reminder`, `nextSendAt` set to July 19th — still fires the day before; only its content needs the new time. Not a new mechanism, just the existing one's content field updated in place before it sends: "10:00 AM" becomes "12:00 PM." The SMS carries the same correction. The schedule doesn't change, only what it says.*

*→ **The confirmation needs drafting — both channels — and approval on each, not just the one.** No existing Action in the A2P spec's library covers "draft a customer-facing confirmation that an appointment changed." The closest, `ACT-CALL-005` — "Draft callback script" — is a script for the rep to read on a call, not something that goes to the customer. This scene proposes a new one, following the same naming convention: `ACT-CALL-009`, "Draft appointment change confirmation (email and/or SMS)" — Draft, human review, `Requires Approval: Yes`, for both. Not locked, not in the spec yet — a gap this scene surfaced, the same way `sms_consent` was.*

*→ Worth being explicit about why SMS doesn't get a pass here even though `sms_consent` is already on file from Chapter 4. Consent answers "can we text her at all" — it doesn't answer "should this specific message go out unread by anyone here first." Sarah reviews both drafts before either sends. Consent isn't an approval shortcut for the channel that happens to already have one recorded.*

*→ Both drafts land in Sarah's queue the same way `ACT-REV-001`'s review-reply draft did, back in the working example this document keeps returning to: `outcome_type: draft_created`, `outcome_status: waiting_for_approval` on each — sitting in the communication log as something to review, not something already done. Plain language, same message both channels: *"Your consultation is confirmed for July 20th at 12:00 PM. We look forward to seeing you."* Nothing promotional, same register as the original guide and the nurture emails. Sarah reads both and approves them within the hour. `outcome_status` updates to `completed`, `human_review_state` to `approved` on each — a real event this time, not an invented one. They go out: an email to Denise's inbox, a text to her phone, both saying the same thing, both confirmed only because Sarah actually read them first, not because a system decided they were fine to send.*

*→ Worth being precise about how the availability check and both confirmation drafts actually end up on the same thread, because it isn't a second round of the AI matching Chapter 5 needed. That matching was real inference: a call arriving cold, days later, with no explicit link to anything — the system had to compute a confidence score just to guess it was related. The availability check and `ACT-CALL-009`'s two drafts aren't in that position at all. They're not independent events discovered later; they're Actions the Orchestrator selected as a direct consequence of processing that same voicemail, inside one continuous Signal Detection → Orchestrator → Action Queue → Execution chain. Whatever step creates the `action_queue`/`action_executions` rows for them simply copies `thread_id` forward from the triggering event — an inheritance, not an inference. No confidence score needed; the system already knows they're related because it created them itself, in the same breath, from the same voicemail.*

*→ That's what makes Chapter 5's reconciliation worth having done at all. Once the voicemail's own `thread_id` was corrected, everything the Orchestrator spawns from it inherits the fix automatically — the availability check, both confirmation drafts, and anything else this chain produces. A support person (or Sarah, reviewing the comm log) pulling up this topic sees one continuous thread: the original booking call, both reminders, the voicemail, the availability check, and the two confirmation drafts — seven touches, one reference. Get the reconciliation wrong at the voicemail, and every downstream inheritance would have carried that mistake forward too; get it right once, and everything spawned after it is correct for free.*

## Chapter 7 — The Job

*→ The signed agreement is the moment `appointment_id` graduates into something bigger — a **project account**, opened against the same `thread_id` this whole saga has been running on since Chapter 3. Everything before this point was about *getting* the job; everything from here is about *running* it. Same reference, same continuous record — a support person pulling up Denise's file doesn't hit a wall at the signature, they keep reading.*

The office sets up the project: scope entered line by line (demo, flooring, vanity, tile, shower, finishing), before photos taken and attached to the project account, and — same AI Visualizer that pre-sale prospects use to imagine a room, pointed the other direction now — a set of renderings generated showing what the finished bathroom will actually look like. Not the pixel, not the IVR, not a one-off nurture send — a **project account** she can log into, with SMS and email both wired to it. Every message either side sends from here lives inside that one thread: photos, questions, updates, invoices. The comm-id principle from Chapters 4–6 reaches its natural end state — instead of reconciling separate touches back to one reference after the fact, the project account *is* the reference, from day one of the job.*

The job finishes on time, on budget within a rounding error, and Denise gets her bathroom. Somewhere in the Cohort 2 network, a Research-bucket, Tier-1, email-only contact who took four months, three nurture touches, one rescheduled consultation, and $20,000 to close is now also a four-phase, 72-hour data point — the next estimate for the next Denise-shaped renovation a little more honest than this one was.

Roughly thirty distinct touchpoints, four months, two devices, three channels, one job.

*→ Here's the part worth being precise about, because what actually crosses into Cohort 2 is broader than a shape with no content in it. Three things travel, not one: the trajectory pattern itself (tier transitions, touches-to-convert, channel path, close value, phase variance — everything the table above already shows); the call and voicemail transcripts, with personal identifiers stripped — names, phone numbers, her street address, anything that would re-identify her — but the substantive content intact, what she actually said, asked, objected to; and the demographic profile — gender, age bracket, household income bracket. That last one isn't AI-inferred from her voice or her words. It's entered by whoever actually met her, James or Sarah, the same way any CRM captures what a rep learned about a client in person — a human judgment call, logged deliberately, not a machine's guess.*

*→ Worth naming honestly what isn't settled here. PII redaction on a transcript — stripping names and numbers while keeping the sentence structure and content around them usable — is a real, buildable step, but nothing in any spec read for this document describes how it actually runs: what gets redacted, what confidence a redaction pass needs before something ships to the network, who reviews it before it leaves RightFlush's system. And nobody in this story tells Denise that a demographic estimate someone formed about her in her own kitchen ends up, de-identified, informing pattern-matching for other contractors' clients in other cities. That's not a technical gap — the mechanism itself is simple — it's a disclosure question nothing here has answered.*

*→ This is also where the two things this document has kept carefully separate — identity and intent, all the way back to Chapter 1 — get drawn precisely, one more time, rather than dissolved. Identity resolution answers "is this a real, known person, and which one" — that answer, and the literal identifiers that produce it, stay in RightFlush's hub profile. Everything else — what she said, what she was worried about, roughly who she is demographically, how her tier and bucket moved — travels, because none of it requires knowing her name to be useful to the network. The redaction step is what does the separating now, not a decision to withhold content altogether.*

*→ What the network actually does with thirty of these, or three thousand, is the part that isn't a single event, it's the whole reason the Orchestrator exists in something other than its launch-day rules table (§10, `clearsky-master-architecture.md`): "across all licensed ClearSky clients, what does the Engagement Score trajectory look like for contacts that converted at this score level with this bucket, what action was taken, did it result in a booked job." One Denise proves nothing. A thousand contacts who all sat quietly at Tier 2B for a week and a half before an email-capture guide converted them — that's a pattern RightFlush's own Orchestrator gets to use on the *next* visitor who looks like early-Denise, weeks before that visitor ever downloads anything. This is the whole shape of the business, not a metaphor for it: **one contractor per trade per market** (§18), and every trajectory pattern in that contractor's Cohort 2 feed works exclusively for them in their geography. Nobody else gets to have learned from this Denise. That's not a side effect of the network — per the same section, it's the thing being sold.*

The next Denise-shaped visitor the Orchestrator sees — different name, different bathroom, maybe a different city in the same protected market — inherits all thirty of these touchpoints, not as her story, but as one more line in a pattern that gets a little more certain every time a real job closes honestly.

## Chapter 9 — What a Good Job Is Worth Twice

A week after the crew leaves, RightFlush asks Denise for a review.

*→ Worth flagging before going further: no Action anywhere in the library actually covers "send a review request after job completion." Not `ACT-REV-*`, not `ACT-CALL-*`, not `ACT-TASK-*`. Every review-handling Action in this system — drafting a reply, marking a testimonial, logging a mismatch — assumes a review already exists (`review_received` is the event type every one of them keys off). Nothing generates the ask itself. This scene assumes RightFlush sends one, the ordinary way any contractor would, because the alternative is worse: no review ever gets left, and nothing downstream in this chapter has anything to fire on.*

She leaves five stars: *"RightFlush was fantastic — showed up when they said they would, kept the place clean, finished on time. Already told my sister to call them for her kitchen."*

*→ `review_received` fires. AI reads it: `rating: 5`, `ai_context.praise_topics: ["punctuality", "cleanliness", "on-time completion"]`, `rating_content_mismatch: false` — the stars and the words agree, no contradiction to flag. Two signals clear their thresholds off the same review, for two different reasons.*

*→ **`SIG-GROW-002` (`testimonial_candidate_detected`)** — rating ≥ 4, praise topics present, confidence ≥ 0.80, no mismatch. All true. This is precisely the review the signal was built to catch.*

*→ **`SIG-GROW-005` (`referral_or_word_of_mouth_detected`)** — "already told my sister to call them" is recommendation language, plain as it gets. Confidence clears 0.75. Also fires.*

*→ Here's a real inconsistency worth naming rather than quietly following: `004_40_signals_seed.sql`'s own mapping table wires **both** of these signals to `ACT-REV-007` (`create_mismatch_review_draft`) as their primary action. Read what that Action actually does (`002_seed_rules.sql`): "Draft a reply for a review where the star rating and review text conflict." That's the mismatch-handling Action — the opposite case of what just happened here, where the rating and the text agree completely. The Action that's actually named for this — `ACT-REV-005`, `mark_testimonial_candidate`, automatic, no approval, built specifically to "flag a positive review as a candidate for use as a testimonial" — isn't wired to `SIG-GROW-002` at all in the seed file. This reads like a copy-paste error from whoever authored the 40-signal expansion, not a deliberate design choice. Worth confirming and fixing, not treating as intentional.*

Sarah sees the testimonial flag land in her queue alongside the referral one. Denise's review — cleanliness, punctuality, on-time finish — becomes a proof point on the before/after gallery page, right next to the photos from her own job.

*→ This is ContentRadar's loop closing all the way back to Chapter 1, from the other direction. That chapter opened with an AI-authored blog post surfacing a demand signal nobody at RightFlush had to notice themselves. This is the same pipeline pointed at supply instead of demand: a real completed job, with real before/after photos and a real five-star review attached, becomes raw material for the next piece of content — the same DataForSEO-and-AI-author process, the same non-negotiable stop before it goes anywhere. Sarah reads it. She approves it. It goes up.*

*→ There's no Action named for turning "referral mentioned in a review" into an actual contact record for the sister — `SIG-GROW-005` maps to the same miswired `ACT-REV-007`, and even corrected to something sensible, nothing in this system's Action Library defines "create a lead from a referral mention." `ACT-TASK-001` — the secondary action already correctly mapped for both signals — covers it well enough for now: a task gets created, someone at RightFlush follows up. A blunter tool than the rest of this system usually reaches for, and worth building the sharper one eventually.*

The sister calls the following week, asking about a kitchen. Nobody ran an ad. Nothing was scored, bucketed, or nurtured to get her there — she arrives already trusting the outcome, because someone she trusts already lived it. She is, from the system's point of view, a brand-new Tier 3 visitor the instant she lands on `/kitchen-renovations` — no fingerprint, no cookie, no history — and the whole seven-chapter loop is ready to start over, for her, exactly as blind to who she is as it was for Denise in February.

## Chapter 10 — Staying Someone Worth Remembering

Denise isn't a lead anymore, and she isn't mid-job anymore either. For everyone the system has been built to chase, converge on, and close, there's no active mechanism for what she is now: closed, happy, and easy to forget about for the next three years unless something deliberately prevents that.

*→ The signal that exists for this is thinner than it sounds. `SIG-GROW-003` (`customer_reactivation_opportunity_detected`) fires off `crm_customer_created` — once, with a 7-day cooldown — and its description is honest about its own scope: "a past customer **may be ready** for seasonal or related service follow-up." It doesn't send anything to Denise. It maps to `ACT-COM-002` — `notify_owner` — an internal notification, full stop. The system's entire built mechanism for "should we reach out to a past customer" is: tell James or Sarah an opportunity might exist, and let a person decide what, if anything, to actually say.*

That's most of why this works instead of feeling like a drip campaign. A holiday text goes out in December — plain, from RightFlush, nothing attached to sell. Around the one-year mark of her renovation, someone checks in: *how's the bathroom holding up?* Sometime that first fall, an invite to follow RightFlush on Facebook. Denise follows the page. RightFlush follows her back — automatically, the same day, nobody at the office has to remember to do it.

*→ Worth pausing on the follow-back specifically, because it's the one piece of this chapter that's actually a deterministic, buildable mechanism, not just a human notification. The pattern matches every other webhook-driven Action in this document: a page-follow event arrives (Meta's Page webhook fires something like `page.follows.added` — the exact field name isn't confirmed anywhere read for this project, only the shape of the pattern), Event Intake normalizes it the same way it normalizes a Telnyx call event, a new signal fires off the condition "customer followed the page, page hasn't followed back yet," and an automatic Action executes the follow-back — no approval needed, no draft, the same low-risk automatic lane `ACT-CALL-004` already runs in.*

*→ One real thing this document can't confirm, and shouldn't paper over: whether Meta's platform actually **lets** a business Page programmatically follow an individual personal profile back. Pages and personal profiles live under different rules on that platform — a Page's relationship to people is normally "followers," not "following," and personal profiles have their own privacy controls over who or what can follow them at all. This isn't a ClearSky architecture question the way the `CallEvent` bridge was; it's a platform-capability question that needs a real answer from Meta's current Graph API and Page policies before anyone builds this, not an assumption made in a story.*

*→ Once (or if) that's confirmed possible, the reciprocal-only rule matters as much as the automation does. RightFlush never goes looking for Denise's profile — the follow-back only fires because she followed first. That ordering is the whole difference between "a business that responds when someone reaches toward them" and "a business watching a customer who never asked to be watched." The same document that wouldn't let a fingerprint substitute for consent, back in Chapter 1, doesn't get to skip that distinction here just because the stakes feel smaller.*

*→ Worth being honest about what's actually being proposed here versus what's built. `ACT-COM-002` firing a notification is real. Everything downstream of that — a human actually drafting a holiday message, an anniversary check-in, the Facebook follow invite itself — has no Action, no template, no schedule anywhere in this system's library. This isn't a gap in the sense the `CallEvent` bridge was a gap — nothing here is broken — it's simply unbuilt, because nothing in any spec read for this document was ever asked to solve "how do we stay in touch with someone after the job is done and there's nothing left to sell them." Chapters 1–9 are the acquisition-through-delivery loop. This is the part after the loop, and it doesn't exist yet as a system — only as a notification and a person who reads it.*

*→ A birthday touch specifically can't be built at all yet, and it's worth being precise about why rather than assuming the data exists somewhere: nothing in any schema read for this document stores a date of birth, or an anniversary date beyond the project's own `completed_at`. If that's wanted, it would need to be captured the same deliberate, manual way the demographic profile was in Chapter 8 — someone who actually met Denise asking, and logging it, not a system inferring or guessing it.*

A year and a half later, a fitting under the new vanity starts weeping. Denise doesn't search anything. She doesn't need ContentRadar to have caught a trend, or a blog post to resurface at the right moment, or a fingerprint to survive a browser update. She still has the number.

*→ This is the whole thesis of the chapter in one call: everything from Chapter 1 onward exists to solve the problem of a stranger who doesn't know you exist yet. None of it is built to solve the much smaller, much cheaper problem of a person who already knows exactly who you are and just needs a reason to remember it before they need you again. The second problem doesn't need a fingerprint, a tier, or a bucket. It needs somebody to have sent a plain, unhurried message in December that didn't ask for anything.*
