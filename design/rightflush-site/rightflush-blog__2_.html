How to prevent frozen pipes in Timmins

Northern Ontario cold snaps can drop to −40°C. Pipes in exterior walls, crawl spaces, and garages are most at risk. Here's what we've learned from 6 years of freeze-related calls in Timmins.

Read post

The three most vulnerable locations

  • Exterior walls. Supply lines running through walls facing north or west are most exposed. Often identifiable because taps on those walls run cold or slow in extreme cold.
  • Under kitchen sinks on exterior walls. Cabinet doors keep the pipe side colder than the room. Open cabinet doors on cold nights.
  • Crawl spaces and basements near the foundation. Cold air infiltration around the water line entry point is a common cause in older Timmins homes.

What to do in a cold snap

Allow cold taps on exterior walls to drip slightly overnight during sustained −30°C periods. The flow prevents a static freeze. It wastes a small amount of water but costs far less than a burst pipe repair and remediation.

Insulate the cabinet under any sink on an exterior wall. Foam pipe insulation from any hardware store takes 10 minutes to install and can make the difference in a serious cold snap.

If a pipe is frozen but hasn't burst

Do not use a heat gun, open flame, or boiling water. These cause rapid thermal expansion that splits the pipe. Use a hair dryer on low heat, moving slowly along the pipe. Start at the tap end and work toward the cold zone. If water doesn't flow within 30 minutes, call us — the freeze may be in an inaccessible location requiring professional thaw equipment.

If a pipe has burst

Shut off the main water valve immediately. Then call (705) 700-1234. This is the single most important action — every second the main stays open adds water damage. We dispatch for burst pipes 24 hours a day.

Tank vs tankless: which is right for a Timmins home?

Tankless water heaters are more efficient — but they're not right for every situation. We break down the real cost difference, installation considerations, and what we actually recommend for Northern Ontario homeowners.

Read post

Tankless systems

A condensing tankless unit (Navien, Rinnai, or Noritz) costs $3,200–$4,800 installed. It heats water on demand — no standby loss, endless hot water, and a 20+ year lifespan. The savings on gas bills typically recover the cost difference in 7–10 years.

The catch: tankless requires adequate gas line sizing (typically ¾" minimum), proper venting (direct-vent or power-vent to exterior), and a dedicated electrical outlet. In many older Timmins homes, these upgrades add to the install cost. We quote the full scope — never just the unit price.

Our actual recommendation

For a household of 2 or fewer with a smaller existing gas supply line: a high-efficiency tank is often the smarter economic choice. For a family of 3 or more with a modern gas supply: tankless delivers real long-term savings. If you're replacing a failed tank urgently, we can install a tank same-day. A tankless conversion requires a planned install with adequate prep time.

We don't push tankless because the margin is better. We recommend what fits your situation. Send us a FotoJobber photo of your existing unit and we'll quote both options clearly.

What does plumbing actually cost in Timmins in 2026?

Plumbing costs vary widely and most contractors don't publish pricing. We do. Real ranges for the most common jobs in Timmins — with the factors that move the number up or down.

Read post

These are real ranges based on our jobs in Timmins over the past 12 months. Prices vary based on access, pipe type, and what we find when we open things up.

Common jobs and what they cost

Dripping tap replacement: $95–$250. Most drips are a worn cartridge or washer — a 30–45 minute repair. Higher end if the valve body needs replacing or if access is awkward.

Blocked drain clearing: $150–$400. Simple kitchen or bathroom block is on the low end. Main line blockage requiring jetting and camera inspection is toward the top. Root intrusion adds time and cost.

Hot water tank replacement (40-gal gas): $1,200–$1,800. Includes removal and disposal of old unit, installation of new tank, all connections, and testing. Brand and access affect the number.

Tankless installation (new): $3,200–$4,800. Includes unit, all venting, gas line work, electrical, and commissioning. Existing infrastructure that needs upgrading is quoted separately.

Bathroom renovation plumbing (full scope): $4,500–$14,000. Rough-in to fixture installation. Wide range because it depends entirely on how much the layout changes and what fixtures are specified.

Emergency callout (burst pipe, flooding): $400–$1,200+. Depends on the repair scope. The emergency response itself is covered by our standard flat-rate — we quote the repair before starting.

What moves the price

Access is the biggest variable. A pipe in an open basement costs less to reach than one behind a finished wall. Old galvanised pipe that crumbles when worked is harder than modern copper or PEX. Permit requirements add cost but protect you — we always recommend permitted work for anything structural.

Annual plumbing maintenance checklist for Timmins homeowners

Most plumbing failures are preventable. A 30-minute walkthrough once a year catches the issues that turn into $2,000 repairs. Here's the checklist we give our customers.

Read post

Water heater

  • Check the temperature setting — 49°C is the recommended safe setting for most households.
  • Look at the pressure relief valve. There should be no mineral deposits or corrosion around it. If there are, it may need replacing.
  • Check the anode rod every 3 years. This sacrificial rod prevents tank corrosion. Replace when more than 50% depleted.

Under every sink

  • Open the cabinet and look for moisture, staining, or corrosion on the P-trap and supply connections.
  • Feel the flexible supply lines — they should be firm, not soft or bulging. Replace any that feel degraded.
  • Run the water and watch the P-trap for drips.

Toilets

  • Add a few drops of food colouring to the tank. If colour appears in the bowl within 15 minutes without flushing, the flapper is leaking — a slow toilet leak can waste thousands of litres per month.
  • Check that the toilet doesn't rock. A loose base can break the wax ring and cause water damage to the subfloor.

Exterior taps and hose bibs

  • Before freeze season, disconnect garden hoses. Trapped water in hoses can freeze back into the interior valve.
  • If your home has interior shutoffs for exterior taps, close them and drain the exterior section.

When to call a plumber

If anything looks wrong or you're unsure — call us. A 15-minute phone consultation is free. Most homeowners spend $800–$2,000 on emergency repairs that could have been a $150 preventive fix. The math always favours the annual checkup.

Why drain cleaners make most blockages worse

Chemical drain cleaners are one of the most misused products in plumbing. We see the damage they cause regularly. Here's when they help, when they don't, and what to do instead.

Read post

Older homes in Timmins often have ABS plastic drain pipe from the 1970s–1990s. Caustic chemicals degrade ABS over time, making it brittle. We regularly open walls and find pipes that look corroded from repeated chemical drain cleaner use. Replacing an ABS drain line costs $600–$1,200. The damage isn't covered by warranty because it's from homeowner-applied chemicals.

What to do instead

  • Slow drain: Try a drain snake (they're $15 at any hardware store). A mechanical obstruction requires mechanical removal.
  • Fully blocked: Call us. We can usually clear a standard drain blockage in 30–45 minutes for $150–$250 — less than the cost of the pipe damage from repeated chemical attempts.
  • Main line backup: Never use chemicals. This needs professional jetting and camera inspection. Call immediately.

The honest answer: chemical drain cleaners have a very narrow use case. When in doubt, call a plumber before reaching for the bottle.

What happens to your plumbing during a bathroom renovation

Most homeowners don't know what the plumber actually does during a bathroom renovation — or how to coordinate the plumbing scope with a general contractor. Here's the full picture.

Read post

Phase 1: Rough-in

The rough-in is everything that happens before the walls are closed. Supply lines are moved or extended to suit the new layout. The drain stack is relocated if the toilet or shower is moving position. New vent connections are made as required by code. A floor drain may be added if a walk-in shower is going in without a tub surround.

Rough-in happens early — after demolition but before framing or insulation. The general contractor needs to frame around the plumber's rough-in locations. This requires good communication and a confirmed layout plan before anyone swings a hammer.

We provide a detailed rough-in drawing that shows exact locations of all supply lines, drains, and vent connections. This is what the GC works from.

Phase 2: Fixture trim-out

Once walls are tiled and floors are laid, we come back for the trim-out: installing the toilet, connecting the vanity, setting the shower valve trim and fixtures, mounting the tub filler if there's a freestanding tub, and connecting the supply lines to all fixtures.

Trim-out typically happens near the end of the renovation — after tile, before final paint and accessories. It's usually 1–2 days depending on complexity.

What you need to confirm before rough-in starts

  • Final position of toilet (affects drain stack)
  • Shower configuration — size, shower head height, whether there are body sprays
  • Vanity height and width (affects supply line placement)
  • Whether a freestanding tub or alcove tub is planned
  • Heated floor zone valve location if applicable

If you change your mind on any of these after rough-in, expect additional cost. Changes to drain locations in particular require opening the subfloor — which the GC has usually closed by then.

Is Timmins water hard? What that means for your home.

Hard water is the single biggest long-term threat to plumbing fixtures and appliances in Timmins. Here's what it does, how to measure it, and what actually fixes it.

Read post

Dishwashers and washing machines. Both run hot water through small internal passages. Hard water scale accumulates in these passages and on heating elements, shortening appliance life.

How to know how hard your water is

Water hardness test kits are available at hardware stores for under $20. We also test water hardness during any service call if requested. If your shower glass has heavy scale or your kettle builds up white deposits quickly, your hardness is likely above 200 mg/L.

What actually fixes it

A whole-home water softener at the main supply is the most effective solution. We install softeners sized to your household water usage. The softener regenerates automatically using salt — the only ongoing maintenance is keeping the salt tank filled. Annual cost for salt is typically $80–$150.

A sediment pre-filter in front of the softener extends the softener resin life and improves overall water quality.

Winterizing your Timmins cottage or camp before the season ends

Leaving water in pipes at a seasonal property through a Northern Ontario winter is a guaranteed burst. Here's exactly how to drain a camp properly so you come back to nothing damaged in spring.

Read post

The complete winterization process

Step 1: Shut off the main water supply. Whether it's a well pump or a municipal supply, the first step is stopping water from entering the system.

Step 2: Open all taps fully. Every hot and cold tap, every shower valve, every laundry connection. Let them drain completely. Start at the highest point in the building and work down.

Step 3: Flush all toilets. Flush until the tank and bowl are both empty. Then pour RV antifreeze into both the tank and the bowl — about 2 cups each. This protects the wax ring seal and any water that remains in the trap.

Step 4: Drain the water heater. Connect a hose to the drain valve at the bottom of the tank. Open the pressure relief valve to allow air in as it drains. A 40-gallon tank holds 150 litres — allow 20–30 minutes to drain fully.

Step 5: Blow out the lines (if you have a compressor). Starting at the furthest point from the main, use compressed air to blow residual water out of each run. This is optional but recommended for camps where temperatures drop below −30°C regularly.

Step 6: Pour antifreeze into every trap. Every drain has a P-trap that holds water as a sewer gas barrier. That water will freeze. Pour 1/2 cup of plumbing-rated RV antifreeze (not automotive antifreeze) into every sink drain, shower drain, and floor drain.

When to hire a plumber to do it

If your camp has a well pump, a pressure tank, or a complex pipe layout — hire a plumber to winterize it properly. A professional winterization costs $200–$400 and is far cheaper than repairing a failed system in the spring.

5 signs your water heater is about to fail

Most water heaters give warning signs months before they fail completely. Catching them early means a planned replacement rather than a Sunday emergency call. Here are the ones to watch for.

Read post

Sign 2: Rusty or discoloured hot water

Rust-coloured water from hot taps only (not cold) indicates internal tank corrosion. The steel lining has failed and the tank is deteriorating. This is a late-stage sign — replacement is imminent. A failed tank can leak catastrophically without much warning once corrosion is advanced.

Sign 3: Rumbling or banging noises

Sediment accumulates on the bottom of tank heaters over time — especially in areas with hard water like Timmins. When the burner fires, it heats water under the sediment layer, causing the rumbling and banging you can hear from across the room. This reduces efficiency and stresses the tank bottom, accelerating failure.

Sign 4: Water pooling around the base

Any moisture around the base of a water heater is a warning sign. It may be a fitting or connection issue — which is a repair — or it may be the tank itself beginning to fail at a seam or corrosion point. Have it assessed immediately. A failing tank can release 150+ litres before you notice.

Sign 5: Inconsistent hot water temperature

If you're getting hot water that's randomly too hot or not hot enough despite not changing settings, the thermostat or thermocouple may be failing. In an older unit, this often signals the beginning of broader system failure. The repair cost versus replacement cost calculation often favours replacement when the unit is already over 10 years old.

Send us a FotoJobber photo of your water heater — model number label visible — and describe the symptoms. We'll tell you honestly whether it's worth repairing or whether replacement is the smarter call.
